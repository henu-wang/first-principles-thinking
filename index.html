What Is First Principles Thinking?

First principles thinking is the practice of actively questioning every assumption about a given problem, then creating new knowledge and solutions from scratch. Rather than reasoning by analogy, you break things down to fundamental truths and build up from there.

"In every systematic inquiry where there are first principles, the knowledge of the objects of that inquiry lies in the knowledge of those first principles."
-- Aristotle, Physics, Book I
🏛

Ancient Roots

Aristotle defined a first principle as "the first basis from which a thing is known." In his Metaphysics, he described it as the most fundamental proposition that cannot be deduced from any other. This approach formed the bedrock of Western philosophy and scientific inquiry for over two millennia.

  • Aristotelian logic and deduction
  • The foundation of empirical science
  • Separating assumptions from axioms
🧠

Method of Doubt

Rene Descartes advanced first principles through his "Method of Doubt" -- systematically doubting everything that could possibly be doubted until arriving at indubitable truths. His famous "Cogito, ergo sum" is a first principle derived from this method of radical skepticism.

  • Systematic skepticism as methodology
  • Rebuilding knowledge from certainty
  • Eliminating inherited assumptions
🚀

Modern Application

Today, innovators like Elon Musk use first principles to challenge assumptions. He questioned why rockets cost $65M by breaking down raw material costs (roughly 2% of the price), revealing massive inefficiencies. As noted in decision frameworks on KeepRule, this approach is universally applicable.

  • From space travel to battery technology
  • Disrupting entrenched industries
  • 10x improvement vs. incremental gains

Reasoning by Analogy vs. First Principles

Most people reason by analogy: they copy what others do with modifications. First principles thinkers strip away assumptions and reconstruct from the ground up.

When to Choose Each Approach

First principles thinking is most valuable for novel problems where existing solutions are clearly suboptimal. For routine decisions, analogy-based reasoning is effective and faster. The KeepRule mental models library helps determine which approach fits your situation.

The Five-Step Framework

Follow this structured process to apply first principles thinking to any problem. Synthesized from Aristotle, Descartes, and modern practitioners.

1

Identify and Define the Problem

Clearly articulate what you are solving. Write it in precise language. Ask: What exactly is the outcome I want? What constraints exist? What does success look like? This process of structured problem definition is fundamental to clear thinking.

2

List All Assumptions

Write down every assumption: cost, time, physics, behavior, market conditions, organizational constraints. Be exhaustive. Challenge yourself: "What am I taking for granted?" Many breakthroughs come from questioning assumptions everyone considers obvious.

3

Question Each Assumption

For every assumption ask: "Is this actually true? Can I prove it? What if the opposite were true?" Use the Socratic method. Separate physical constraints (laws of physics) from artificial ones (regulations, conventions, habits). Many "impossible" problems become solvable when you realize the constraint was artificial.

4

Identify Fundamental Truths

After stripping away questionable assumptions, you are left with fundamental truths -- the first principles. In physics: laws of thermodynamics. In business: unit economics. In investment decision-making: cash flow fundamentals and margin of safety.

5

Build Up New Solutions

Using only verified first principles, construct new solutions from scratch. Do not reference existing solutions during this phase. Then compare your first-principles solution with existing approaches. The gap represents untapped opportunity.

Masters of First Principles

Throughout history, the greatest breakthroughs came from those who dared to question conventional wisdom and reason from fundamentals.

Aristotle (384-322 BCE)

Philosopher & Scientist

The originator of "first principles" in Western philosophy. He proposed that understanding any subject requires identifying its most basic elements -- foundational truths that cannot be deduced from anything else.

His approach through systematic observation and logical deduction laid the groundwork for the scientific method. Every branch of Western science traces its methodology back to Aristotle.

PhilosophyLogicScience

Rene Descartes (1596-1650)

Philosopher & Mathematician

His "Meditations on First Philosophy" introduced the method of radical doubt -- discarding any belief that could possibly be false. Only what survives this scrutiny qualifies as a first principle.

His approach produced not only "I think, therefore I am" but also the Cartesian coordinate system and analytic geometry -- unifying algebra and geometry in a way that seemed impossible to contemporaries.

Elon Musk (1971-)

Entrepreneur & Engineer

When told rocket launches cost $65M, Musk asked: "What are the raw material costs?" Answer: roughly 2% of the price. This gap between material costs and market price revealed a massive opportunity that became SpaceX.

Applied the same thinking to EV batteries, boring tunnels, and solar energy. Each time, first principles revealed existing solutions were orders of magnitude more expensive than physics required.

Richard Feynman (1918-1988)

Physicist & Nobel Laureate

Feynman insisted on deriving results himself rather than memorizing formulas. His "Feynman Technique" -- explaining concepts in simple language -- is essentially a first principles exercise.

When investigating the Challenger disaster, he went straight to fundamentals: dropping an O-ring in ice water on live TV, demonstrating the root cause in seconds. Learn more about this approach at KeepRule.

Johannes Gutenberg (c.1400-1468)

Inventor & Craftsman

Gutenberg did not invent any single component. He broke down the problem of mass-producing text into first principles: movable type, adhering ink, and a pressing mechanism.

By combining existing technologies (wine press, oil-based ink, metal alloy type) guided by fundamental requirements, he created one of history's most transformative inventions through combinatorial innovation.

Warren Buffett (1930-)

Investor & Business Thinker

While others follow market trends, Buffett asks: "What is this business actually worth based on fundamentals?" His concept of intrinsic value is a first principle of investing.

His "circle of competence" and "margin of safety" cut through market noise. Explore Buffett's investment principles on KeepRule for detailed analysis of these foundational concepts.

Real-World Examples by Domain

First principles thinking applies across every field. Detailed examples from business, science, and engineering.

Business: Southwest Airlines

Business

When the airline industry assumed you needed hub-and-spoke routing, assigned seating, and meals, Southwest asked: "What do passengers fundamentally need?" Answer: to get from A to B safely, affordably, and on time. Stripping away everything else created the low-cost carrier revolution.

  • First principle: Transportation from A to B
  • Eliminated: Assigned seats, meals, hub routing
  • Result: 47 consecutive years of profitability

Science: CRISPR Gene Editing

Science

Doudna and Charpentier reasoned from the principle that DNA is a sequence of nucleotides that can be cut and rewritten. Rather than working within existing gene therapy limitations, they asked: "What is the simplest way to edit a specific DNA sequence?" The answer was CRISPR -- a natural bacterial defense mechanism repurposed as a precision tool.

  • First principle: DNA is editable chemistry
  • Questioned: Need for complex viral vectors
  • Result: Nobel Prize, revolution in medicine

Engineering: Tesla Battery Packs

Engineering

In 2006, battery packs cost $600/kWh. Musk applied first principles: "What do the raw materials cost on commodity markets?" Roughly $80/kWh. The gap represented manufacturing inefficiency, not material reality. Today batteries approach that theoretical floor, enabling the EV revolution.

  • First principle: Material costs set the floor
  • Questioned: $600/kWh as inevitable
  • Result: Battery costs dropped 90% in 15 years

Investing: Value Investing

Finance

Graham and Buffett built value investing on first principles: a stock is a share of a business worth the discounted sum of future cash flows. This cuts through speculation and herd behavior. The margin of safety principle applies first principles directly to investing decisions.

  • First principle: Intrinsic value = future cash flows
  • Questioned: Market price = true value
  • Result: Decades of outperformance

Architecture: Passive House Design

Architecture

Rather than accepting that buildings need large heating systems, passive house designers asked: "What does a building fundamentally need to maintain temperature?" Answer: insulation, airtightness, heat recovery, and solar orientation. Optimizing these principles reduces heating energy by 90%.

  • First principle: Heat flows from hot to cold
  • Questioned: Need for large HVAC systems
  • Result: 90% energy reduction

Education: Khan Academy

Education

Sal Khan asked: "What does learning fundamentally require?" Clear explanations, practice problems, and immediate feedback -- not expensive campuses or fixed schedules. Rebuilding education from these principles made world-class education free to anyone with internet access.

  • First principle: Learning = explanation + practice
  • Questioned: Need for physical classrooms
  • Result: 150+ million learners worldwide

How to Practice First Principles Thinking

Like any skill, first principles thinking improves with deliberate practice. Concrete techniques for daily use.

The "Five Whys" Technique

Developed at Toyota, this drills down to root causes. Ask "Why?" five times in succession; each answer becomes the next subject. By the fifth iteration, you typically reach a fundamental truth invisible at the surface. Works for business problems and personal decisions alike.

The Blank Page Exercise

Write your problem at the top of a blank page. Without referencing existing solutions, list only fundamental requirements. What must be true for a solution to work? What are the physics, economics, human needs? Build a solution from these alone. Compare with existing approaches to find gaps and opportunities.

Assumption Auditing

Create two columns: "Assumptions" and "Verified Truths." List everything you believe about the situation. For each assumption ask: "How do I know? What evidence supports it?" Move only well-evidenced items to "Verified." You will be surprised how many "facts" are unexamined assumptions waiting to be challenged.

Cross-Domain Learning

Study solutions from completely different fields. How does biology solve distribution? How does physics handle optimization? Understanding first principles across domains builds a toolkit of fundamental patterns applicable anywhere. KeepRule's decision frameworks catalog these cross-domain principles for practical use.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Analysis Paralysis: Reserve first principles thinking for high-impact problems where conventional approaches are clearly failing.

Ignoring Valid Precedent: Sometimes existing solutions are good because they have been refined over time. First principles should complement experience, not replace it.

Overconfidence: Deriving something from first principles does not guarantee correctness. Validate conclusions empirically before committing resources.
